STAT 109: Introductory Biostatistics

Lectures

Below each link: a topic line (matches or summarizes the lecture heading) and outcomes—what you should be able to do after that session. Full detail, activities, and notation stay on the lecture page.

Week 1

Monday

Holiday - No class

Wednesday

Lecture 1

Introduction — instructor, classmates, course, and what statistics is

  • Meet the teacher and your classmates.
  • Describe what STAT 109 covers and how lecture, lab, quizzes, and projects fit together.
  • Explain informally what statistics is and give examples of questions it can help answer.
  • Describe five main steps in the statistical process

Friday

Lecture 2

Probability as data vs theory

  • Compute counts, proportions, and percentages for an event across repeated trials.
  • Use symbols \(x\), \(n\), \(\hat{p}\), and \(P(\text{event})\) for data vs theoretical probability.
  • Distinguish observed outcomes from theoretical probability in a random process.
  • State three rules that probabilities must satisfy.
  • Define probability and connect it to long-run relative frequency.

Week 2

Monday

Lecture 3

Probability calculations the long way — sample spaces and events

  • Define and identify sample space, outcome, and event for a trial.
  • Use notation \(P(A)\) for the probability of an event.
  • State and use the formula for \(P(A)\) when outcomes in the sample space are equally likely.

Wednesday

Lecture 4

Random babies — probability by careful counting

  • List the sample space for randomly returning four babies to four mothers.
  • Count outcomes for events such as “all correct” or “exactly \(k\) correct.”
  • Compute probabilities when each pairing in the sample space is equally likely.
  • Compare exact probabilities to a simulation (e.g. Random Babies applet).

Friday

Lecture 5

Addition rule for probability

  • State the addition rule for any two events \(A\) and \(B\).
  • Use the addition rule to compute \(P(A \text{ or } B)\), including overlapping events.
  • Apply special cases: complement rule and non-overlapping events.

Week 3

Monday

Lecture 6

Conditional probability and multiplication rule

  • State the definition of conditional probability and compute \(P(A \mid B)\).
  • Use the multiplication rule to compute \(P(A \text{ and } B)\).
  • Decide whether two events are independent using equivalent criteria.

Wednesday

Lecture 7

Law of total probability and Bayes’ rule

  • State the law of total probability for a partition of the sample space.
  • State Bayes’ rule and relate the pieces to conditional and marginal probabilities.
  • Use tree diagrams to organize two-stage problems and find \(P(B)\) and \(P(A \mid B)\).

Friday

Lecture 8

Practice — Lectures 6–7 (diagnostic testing and Bayes)

  • Compute and interpret conditional probabilities from counts (e.g. sensitivity, false positive rate).
  • Assess independence in context and use the law of total probability for \(P(B)\).
  • Build tree diagrams and apply Bayes’ rule to find inverse probabilities such as \(P(C \mid T^+)\).

Week 4

Monday

Lecture 9

Permutations and combinations

  • Distinguish settings where order matters (permutations) from where it does not (combinations).
  • Use \(_nP_r\) and \(_nC_r\) (or \(\binom{n}{r}\)) to solve counting problems.
  • Relate factorials to the permutation and combination formulas.

Wednesday

Lecture 10

From coin tosses to the binomial random process

  • Define a discrete random variable and its probability distribution (pdf).
  • Define a binomial random variable and a binomial random process.
  • Match notation (\(n\), \(x\), \(p\)) and the four conditions for a binomial process to a coin-toss setting.
  • Express \(P(X = x)\) using the binomial pdf.

Friday

Lecture 11

Practice — binomial random process

  • Decide whether a scenario satisfies the four binomial conditions and justify your answer.
  • Identify \(n\), \(p\), and the random variable \(X\) for a binomial count.
  • Write and evaluate probabilities using the binomial pdf; use mean \(\mu = np\) and SD \(\sigma = \sqrt{np(1-p)}\) as needed.

Week 5

Monday

Lecture 12

Introduction to the binomial test (ESP example)

  • Translate a research question about a proportion into \(H_0\) and \(H_a\).
  • Check the four BRP conditions for data from a fixed number of binary trials.
  • Connect the ESP activity to the structure of Project 1.

Wednesday

Lecture 13

ESP worksheet — binomial test in practice

  • State hypotheses and verify the BRP conditions for your own ESP test data.
  • Identify \(n\) and \(p\) under \(H_0\) and record your observed count \(x\).
  • Obtain a p-value from the sample R notebook and share results as directed.

Friday

Lecture 14

Binomial test — steps and worked examples

  • Carry out the full workflow: question, parameter, hypotheses, \(n\) and \(p\) under \(H_0\), BRP check, null distribution plot, observed \(x\), shading by \(H_a\), p-value, and conclusion in context.
  • Handle one-sided and two-sided alternatives correctly when shading and interpreting the p-value.

Week 6

Monday

Lecture 15

Mean and standard deviation of a binomial random variable

  • Use \(\mu = np\) and \(\sigma = \sqrt{np(1-p)}\) for \(X \sim \text{Binomial}(n,p)\).
  • Interpret the mean as the center and the SD as typical deviation of \(X\).
  • Identify \(n\) and \(p\) in applied settings and compute \(\mu\) and \(\sigma\).

Wednesday

Lecture 16

Normal approximation to the binomial; one-proportion test by hand

  • Use the CLT to approximate a binomial distribution by a normal curve with the same \(\mu\) and \(\sigma\).
  • Check conditions such as \(np \geq 10\) and \(n(1-p) \geq 10\) when appropriate.
  • State the Empirical Rule for normal distributions.
  • Sketch a rejection region under a normal curve for a one-proportion test and relate the shaded area to the p-value (approximate).

Friday

Lecture 17

Type I and Type II errors; power

  • Define Type I and Type II errors and describe each in context.
  • Define power as \(1 - P(\text{Type II error})\) and relate it to detecting a false \(H_0\).
  • Explain how significance level \(\alpha\) and sample size help balance error rates and power.

Week 7

Monday

Lecture 18

Sampling distributions and estimation

  • Define the sampling distribution of a statistic.
  • Describe confidence level, parameter, and the endpoints of a confidence interval in context.
  • Compute and interpret a confidence interval for a population proportion and for a population mean (methods from lecture).

Wednesday

Review

Review before Midterm 1

  • Consolidate probability rules, counting, binomial models, and binomial tests.
  • Bring questions about practice problems and course structure for the exam.

Friday

Midterm 1

Midterm 1 (in lecture)

  • Demonstrate understanding of material covered through the review session (see course schedule and practice materials).

Week 8

Monday

Lecture 19

Confidence intervals and the importance of sampling

  • Discuss bias and its impact given how a sample was collected.
  • Identify ways to reduce bias and increase precision.
  • Identify a sampling frame and carry out simple random sampling.
  • Classify methods as convenience, SRS, cluster, systematic, stratified, or multi-stage.

Wednesday

Lecture 20

Sampling methods in practice

  • Identify a sampling frame for a stated population.
  • Carry out simple random sampling using a numbered list and random draws.
  • Classify sampling designs and discuss bias, generalizability, and how to improve the design.

Friday

Lecture 21

Target & Rubberband Analogy
