Introduction

What is the mean age of high schools in California? As someone who teaches college-level classes, I was interested in learning more about the population of schools my students might come from. One question is how old those schools tend to be: I think older schools might tend to have more resources and stronger connections with a community, whereas newer schools might have less resources.

Parameter: mean age (in years, in 2024) of all California high schools.
Population: high schools listed in the California School Directory.

Methods

Data collection

On April 3, 2024, I visited www.cde.ca.gov/SchoolDirectory and searched for any type of high school. I found \(N = 1617\) schools.

I used the random number generator on RossmanChance.com to choose \(n = 10\) numbers from 1 to 1617. I located each corresponding school by setting the number of schools shown on a page to 100, going to the page with the right hundreds value, and counting up or down to find the school matching each random number’s hundreds and ones digits.

For each of the 10 schools, I looked up the Open Date and calculated age in 2024 by subtracting the open year from 2024. My variable is numerical (age in years).

Data analysis

I used the survey package in R to compute a 95% confidence \(t\)-interval with finite population correction for the mean age of all CA high schools, using the ages from my sample of 10 schools. Step-by-step code is in the mean notebook.

Conditions for this interval:

  • a simple random sample from a numbered list of the population, and
  • belief that the population distribution of ages is roughly symmetric (checked with a histogram).

Results

Descriptive statistics

The mean age of the ten schools was 35 years and the standard deviation was 25 years. See Figure 1 in the sample PDF (histogram of ages).

Inferential statistics

Based on my dataset, I am 95% confident that the mean age of all CA high schools is between 20 and 51 years old in 2024.

Conclusion

The mean age of high schools in CA was younger than I expected. Because my sample of 10 schools was chosen with a simple random sampling scheme, I do not expect my estimate to be biased. However, \(n = 10\) is small, so the estimate is not very precise: there is about a 31-year spread between the lower and upper bounds of the interval. I could improve precision by increasing the sample size.

## Introduction

What is the **mean age** of high schools in California? As someone who teaches college-level classes, I was interested in learning more about the population of schools my students might come from. One question is how old those schools tend to be: I think older schools might tend to have more resources and stronger connections with a community, whereas newer schools might have less resources.

**Parameter:** mean age (in years, in 2024) of all California high schools.  
**Population:** high schools listed in the California School Directory.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Methods

### Data collection

On April 3, 2024, I visited [www.cde.ca.gov/SchoolDirectory](https://www.cde.ca.gov/SchoolDirectory) and searched for any type of high school. I found **$N = 1617$** schools.

I used the [random number generator on RossmanChance.com](https://www.rossmanchance.com/applets/2021/randomnumbers/GenRandom01.htm) to choose **$n = 10$** numbers from 1 to 1617. I located each corresponding school by setting the number of schools shown on a page to 100, going to the page with the right hundreds value, and counting up or down to find the school matching each random number’s hundreds and ones digits.

For each of the 10 schools, I looked up the **Open Date** and calculated **age in 2024** by subtracting the open year from 2024. My variable is **numerical** (age in years).

### Data analysis

I used the **`survey`** package in R to compute a **95% confidence $t$-interval with finite population correction** for the mean age of all CA high schools, using the ages from my sample of 10 schools. Step-by-step code is in the [mean notebook](project-2-estimation-mean-fpc.ipynb).

Conditions for this interval:

- a **simple random sample** from a numbered list of the population, and  
- belief that the **population distribution of ages is roughly symmetric** (checked with a histogram).

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Results

### Descriptive statistics

The **mean age** of the ten schools was **35 years** and the **standard deviation** was **25 years**. See **Figure 1** in the [sample PDF](project-2-sample.pdf) (histogram of ages).

### Inferential statistics

Based on my dataset, I am **95% confident** that the **mean age of all CA high schools** is between **20 and 51 years old** in 2024.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Conclusion

The mean age of high schools in CA was **younger than I expected**. Because my sample of 10 schools was chosen with a **simple random sampling** scheme, I do not expect my estimate to be **biased**. However, **$n = 10$** is small, so the estimate is **not very precise**: there is about a **31-year** spread between the lower and upper bounds of the interval. I could improve precision by **increasing the sample size**.
