Project 1: Testing a hypothesis from a binomial random process

Total points: 35
Due: Sunday, Week 5 (see Assessments and Canvas)

In this project, you will write a one-page summary of how you used the binomial distribution to draw a conclusion about an infinite process from data you collected. For the inference to be valid, your data must satisfy the four conditions of a binomial random process (BRP). The steps below guide you through planning, collecting data, analyzing in R, and writing the report.

Sample write-up: Download sample one-page write-up — ESP binomial test. This is my completed report for an ESP study. Do not copy my process (ESP is a class example you may not use for your own project). Write up your own study following the grading rubric below.

Steps

Please follow these steps before Thursday, Feb. 19:

  1. THINK of a binomial random process you could observe by Thursday, Feb. 19. Carefully consider each of the four BRP conditions until you are sure each holds for your chosen process. Do not use a process from a class lecture example, such as coin tosses or ESP. The four conditions are: (a) binary outcome in each trial (success or failure), (b) independent outcomes between trials, (c) fixed probability of success in a single trial (\(p\)), and (d) fixed number of trials (\(n\)).

  2. BEFORE you observe the process, make your best guess about whether \(p\), the probability of one success in a single trial, is larger than, smaller than, or not equal to some number. Write your guess as null and alternative hypotheses:

    \(H_0\): \(p\) = some number
    \(H_a\): \(p\) (> or < or \(\neq\)) some number

    The value for \(p\) must be a number between 0 and 1.

    Be ready to share your two hypotheses in class Thursday, Feb. 19 so you can make a plot of what to expect (step 4) in lab.

  3. OBSERVE \(n = 25\) trials from your chosen process. Record how many successes occurred (\(x\)). Compute the observed proportion \(\hat{p} = x/n\).

  4. In R, use dbinom() and geom_col() to create a bar plot of \(P(X = x)\) for \(x = 0, 1, 2, \ldots, n\) using your \(n\) and the value of \(p\) from \(H_0\). Use geom_col() with exact binomial probabilities from dbinom(), not geom_bar() on simulated data.

  5. Add a vertical line at your observed \(x\) with geom_vline(xintercept = ...). Compute the p-value: the probability of observing a new value of \(X\) as extreme as your \(x\), assuming \(H_0\) is true. Use pbinom() (or equivalent) and match the direction of your alternative hypothesis.

  6. Based on the size of your p-value, decide whether \(H_0\) is consistent with your data. A small p-value means your observed \(x\) is far from where new data would most likely fall if your \(H_0\) value of \(p\) were correct.

    You will have time in class on Thursday, Feb. 19 to create your plot and p-value. See also Lab 5 (precheck) and the Lecture 12 binomial test Colab notebook.

  7. Write up your study in a one-page summary using the section headings in the rubric below. Include your R figures (descriptive bar/pie chart and binomial distribution plot with your data marked).

Project 1: One page summary of binomial test — grading rubric

Format (2 pts)

Your summary should be organized into 4 sections (1 section with 2 subsections) with the following headings:

  1. Introduction
  2. Data Collection
  3. Data Analysis
    • 3a. Descriptive Statistics
    • 3b. Inferential Statistics
  4. Conclusion

Style (3 pts)

  • Your summary should fit within one side of an 8.5 by 11 inch page. (1 point)
  • Your summary should be concise and written in complete sentences with proper grammar. (2 points)

Introduction section (5 pts)

In this section, you should:

  1. Briefly describe what question you are trying to answer. (1 point)
  2. Briefly describe the motivation you have for trying to answer the question. (1 point)
  3. Describe the parameter of interest \(p\) in one sentence. (1 point)
  4. State the null and alternative hypotheses for your research question using appropriate notation and a value for \(p\). (2 points)

Data collection section (7 pts)

  • Describe how, when, and where you collected data in enough detail that someone else could repeat your study. (3 points)
  • Explicitly explain why you believe each of the four BRP conditions was satisfied by your method. (4 points) Explain in the context of your study, not as a generic list of the four conditions.

Descriptive statistics subsection (6 pts)

  • Include the total number of trials \(n\), the number of successes \(x\), and the percentage of successes you observed. (3 points)
  • Include a well-labelled pie chart or bar chart of your observed data, made in R (e.g. with ggplot2). (3 points)

Inferential statistics subsection (8 pts)

In this subsection, please include:

  • A graph of the binomial distribution for all possible values of \(X\) using the \(p\) from \(H_0\). Create this as a column chart in R using dbinom() for the bar heights and geom_col(). (4 points)
  • The p-value: the probability of seeing a new \(X\) as extreme as your \(x\) if \(H_0\) is true. Compute it in R with pbinom() (or an equivalent correct method). (2 points)
  • A brief explanation of how you calculated the p-value. (1 point)
  • A clear indication of where your data \(x\) appears on the binomial graph (e.g. a vertical line or label). (1 point)

See the ESP sample PDF for an example of wording and figures.

Conclusion section (4 pts)

  • Write a one-sentence summary of what your p-value means in the context of your research question and data. (2 points) (See the sample project for an example.)
  • Based on the size of this p-value, state what you conclude about your research question. (2 points)
