Quiz 7 Practice Problems

These practice problems cover Lecture 17: Type I and Type II errors, power, and balancing errors in hypothesis testing. You should be able to state null and alternative hypotheses, define Type I and Type II errors in context, interpret decisions when a p-value and significance level are given, and know how to reduce each type of error.

Instructions

  • Work without notes first; then check your answers against the solutions in Canvas.
  • The in-person quiz in lab will be created from these practice problems.

Part A: Definitions

A1. Significance level

Define the significance level (often denoted \(\alpha\)) in the context of hypothesis testing.

A2. Type I error

Define a Type I error in the context of hypothesis testing.

A3. Type II error

Define a Type II error in the context of hypothesis testing.

Part B: State hypotheses and define Type I and Type II errors in context

For each scenario below: (1) Write the null hypothesis \(H_0\) and alternative hypothesis \(H_a\) (in symbols and/or words, as appropriate). (2) In context, what would a Type I error be? (3) In context, what would a Type II error be?

B1. Lucky coin

We want to know if a coin lands on heads more often than not (a “lucky” coin for heads). Let \(p\) = probability of heads on a single flip.

  1. State \(H_0\) and \(H_a\).
  2. What would a Type I error be in this context?
  3. What would a Type II error be in this context?

B2. Lucky die

We want to know if a die lands on the face “6” more often than it should. Let \(p\) = probability of a 6 on a single roll.

  1. State \(H_0\) and \(H_a\).
  2. What would a Type I error be in this context?
  3. What would a Type II error be in this context?

B3. Medical treatment effectiveness

A new medical treatment is studied. We want to know if it is effective in more than 10% of cases. Let \(p\) = proportion of patients for whom the treatment is effective.

  1. State \(H_0\) and \(H_a\).
  2. What would a Type I error be in this context?
  3. What would a Type II error be in this context?

B4. Left-handed students

We want to know if the proportion of left-handed students at our school is higher than the national rate of 10%. Let \(p\) = proportion of students at our school who are left-handed.

  1. State \(H_0\) and \(H_a\).
  2. What would a Type I error be in this context?
  3. What would a Type II error be in this context?

B5. Defective items from a supplier

We want to know if the proportion of defective items from a new supplier is less than 15%. Let \(p\) = proportion of items from this supplier that are defective.

  1. State \(H_0\) and \(H_a\).
  2. What would a Type I error be in this context?
  3. What would a Type II error be in this context?

Part C: Given hypotheses, data, and p-value — could Type I or Type II be made?

For each problem, the null and alternative hypotheses and data are given. A binomial test was performed and the p-value is reported. The cutoff for significance is \(\alpha = 0.05\). Answer: (1) Do we reject or fail to reject \(H_0\)? (2) Could a Type I error be being made? (Explain briefly.) (3) Could a Type II error be being made? (Explain briefly.) (4) In context, what would the Type I error (or Type II error, whichever is possible) be?

C1. Rejecting the null

Research question: Is the coin biased toward heads?
Hypotheses: \(H_0: p = 0.5\) vs \(H_a: p > 0.5\), where \(p\) = probability of heads.
Data: We flip the coin 100 times and observe 62 heads.
P-value from binomial test: 0.0096.

  1. Do we reject or fail to reject \(H_0\) at \(\alpha = 0.05\)?
  2. Could a Type I error be being made? Why or why not?
  3. Could a Type II error be being made? Why or why not?
  4. In context, what would the Type I error be (i.e., what mistaken conclusion would we have made)?

C2. Failing to reject the null

Research question: Is the proportion of left-handed students at our school higher than the national rate of 10%?
Hypotheses: \(H_0: p = 0.10\) vs \(H_a: p > 0.10\), where \(p\) = proportion of students at our school who are left-handed.
Data: In a random sample of 25 students, 4 are left-handed.
P-value from binomial test: 0.21.

  1. Do we reject or fail to reject \(H_0\) at \(\alpha = 0.05\)?
  2. Could a Type I error be being made? Why or why not?
  3. Could a Type II error be being made? Why or why not?
  4. In context, what would the Type II error be (i.e., what mistaken conclusion would we have made)?

C3. Another decision

Research question: Is the proportion of defective items from the new supplier less than 15%?
Hypotheses: \(H_0: p = 0.15\) vs \(H_a: p < 0.15\), where \(p\) = proportion of items from this supplier that are defective.
Data: In a random sample of 40 items, 3 are defective.
P-value from binomial test: 0.034.

  1. Do we reject or fail to reject \(H_0\) at \(\alpha = 0.05\)?
  2. Could a Type I error be being made? Why or why not?
  3. Could a Type II error be being made? Why or why not?
  4. In context, what would the Type I error be?

Part D: Multiple choice — reducing Type I and Type II error

D1. Reducing Type I error

Suppose we want to reduce the probability of a Type I error (rejecting the null when it is actually true). Which of the following would help?

A. Use a larger significance level (e.g. \(\alpha = 0.10\) instead of 0.05).
B. Use a smaller significance level (e.g. \(\alpha = 0.01\) instead of 0.05).
C. Increase the sample size.
D. Decrease the sample size.

Answer: _____

D2. Reducing Type II error (when \(\alpha\) is fixed)

Assume the Type I error rate (significance level \(\alpha\)) has been set and we do not change it. What can we do to reduce the Type II error rate (i.e., increase power)?

A. Use a smaller significance level (e.g. \(\alpha = 0.01\) instead of 0.05).
B. Use a larger significance level (e.g. \(\alpha = 0.10\) instead of 0.05).
C. Increase the sample size.
D. Decrease the sample size.

Answer: _____
