Quiz 12

Quiz 12 practice problems

These practice problems cover Lecture 31 (study design and inference language: random sample vs convenience sample; randomized experiment vs observational study; causation, confounding, and chance), Lecture 32 (one-way ANOVA concepts and interpreting overall F-test output), and Lecture 33 (chi-square test of independence concepts and interpreting overall test output).

This quiz is concepts only: no hand-calculation is required.

Instructions

  • Work without notes first, then check your work.
  • For multiple-choice items, choose the best single answer.
  • Unless a question says otherwise, interpret significance at alpha = 0.05.

Part A: Independence in study design (paired or not paired)

For each scenario, classify whether observations are paired/dependent or independent/not paired.

A1.

Thirty students have their resting heart rate measured before and after a 6-week exercise program. Their measurements before and after are:

  1. Paired/dependent by student
  2. Independent/not paired within students

A2.

Two separate sections of STAT 109 take different versions of a quiz. Each student appears in only one section. The quiz scores are:

  1. Paired/dependent between the sections
  2. Independent/not paired between the sections

A3.

Researchers match each smoker with one nonsmoker of similar age and sex, then compare lung function between matched pairs. The measurements of lung function are:

  1. Paired/dependent between the groups of smokers and non-smokers
  2. Independent/not paired between the groups of smokers and non-smokers

A4.

Plant height is measured for seedlings randomly assigned to fertilizer A, B, or C, with each seedling receiving only one fertilizer. The plant height data is:

  1. Paired/dependent between the fertilizer types
  2. Independent/not paired between the fertilizer types

Part B: Descriptive evidence for ANOVA and chi-square

B1. One-way ANOVA visual evidence

Suppose boxplots are used to compare blood-pressure reduction across three treatments. The medians are visibly different, and one group is shifted noticeably higher than the other two.

Which statement is best?

  1. The boxplot is descriptive evidence of at least one group mean difference.
  2. The boxplot proves all population means are different.
  3. The boxplot shows no evidence of any differences.

B2. One-way ANOVA overall p-value from R output

You fit fit <- lm(y ~ group, data = dat) and run anova(fit). The output shows a p-value = 0.012.

At alpha = 0.05, which interpretation is best?

  1. Reject H0; the data provide evidence that at least one population mean differs beyond chance variation under the model assumptions.
  2. Fail to reject H0; all means are exactly equal.
  3. Reject H0; therefore every pair of means is significantly different.

B3. Chi-square descriptive evidence

A 100% stacked bar chart shows disease status (yes/no) by exposure group (low/medium/high). The segment proportions differ clearly across exposure groups.

Which statement is best?

  1. This is descriptive evidence variables may not be independent.
  2. This proves causation by exposure.
  3. This shows the two variables must be independent.

B4. Chi-square overall p-value from R output

You run chisq.test(tab) and see p-value = 0.18.

At alpha = 0.05, which conclusion is best?

  1. Reject H0; there is strong evidence of association.
  2. Fail to reject H0; sample data are compatible with independence (not enough evidence of association).
  3. Accept H0 as true with certainty.

Part C: Interpreting R code output (conceptual)

C1. ANOVA null and alternative

For a one-way ANOVA with 4 groups, which is the correct null?

  1. H0: all four population means are equal.
  2. H0: all four sample means are equal.
  3. H0: at least one population mean differs.

C2. Chi-square test null and alternative

For a chi-square test of independence between treatment arm and side-effect category:

  1. H0: treatment arm and side-effect category are independent in the population.
  2. H0: treatment arm causes side effects.
  3. H0: every cell count in the sample table is equal.

C3. Interpreting overall p-value language

Complete the statement:

“A small p-value means that, if the null were true, data this extreme would be _______.”

  1. likely
  2. uncommon (unlikely)
  3. impossible

C4. “By chance” language

Which sentence is best statistical wording when p = 0.003 for an ANOVA overall test?

  1. “There is strong evidence against equal means; such differences would be unlikely by chance alone if all means were equal.”
  2. “The null is impossible and chance never plays a role.”
  3. “This proves treatment mechanism with no assumptions.”

Part D: Generalizing to a population (sampling design)

D1.

A researcher recruits volunteers from people walking into one gym between 5-6 pm and surveys protein intake.

Can the results be safely generalized to all adults in the city?

  1. Yes, because sample size is all that matters.
  2. Not reliably; this is a convenience sample and may not represent the city population.
  3. Yes, because any sample supports broad generalization.

D2.

A simple random sample (SRS) is drawn from a complete city resident list.

Which statement is best?

  1. Generalization to that city population is as safe as we can manage from limited information
  2. No generalization is ever possible, even from SRS.
  3. SRS automatically proves causation.

Part E: Causation, confounding, and chance (study type)

E1.

In an RCT, participants are randomly assigned to drug vs placebo. The drug group has a lower mean symptom score and a hypothesis test comparing the population means has a very small p-value.

Which interpretation is best?

  1. Random assignment helps support a causal interpretation
  2. Because chance exists, random assignment gives no advantage.
  3. RCTs cannot address causation.

E2.

In an observational study, soda intake is positively associated with BMI.

Which interpretation is best?

  1. The association could reflect causation, confounding or chance; causation is not established by this design alone.
  2. Observational association proves soda causes higher BMI.
  3. Confounding is impossible in observational studies.

E3.

A convenience sample observational study reports a significant association (p < 0.01) between supplement use and fewer colds.

Best interpretation:

  1. Significant p-value alone proves causal effect for everyone.

  2. Evidence of association in the sampled data, but external validity and confounding remain concerns.

  3. Because p is small, sampling design no longer matters.
