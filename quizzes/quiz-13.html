Quiz 13

Quiz 13 practice problems

These practice problems cover Lecture 35 and Lecture 36:

  • reading scatterplots for form, direction, and strength,
  • Pearson correlation and simple linear regression,
  • interpreting slope/intercept and model predictions,
  • residuals, training vs test MSE and R^2,
  • interpreting confidence intervals and prediction intervals from R output.

This quiz is primarily conceptual and interpretation-focused (no long hand calculations).

Instructions

  • Work without notes first, then check your work.
  • For multiple-choice questions, choose the best single answer.
  • Unless stated otherwise, interpret intervals at 95% confidence.

Part A: Scatterplot and correlation concepts

A1.

A scatterplot of y vs x shows points roughly around an upward straight-line trend with moderate scatter.

Best description:

  1. Linear, negative, weak
  2. Linear, positive, moderate
  3. Curved, positive, strong

A2.

Which value of Pearson correlation r best matches a strong negative linear association?

  1. r = -0.86
  2. r = 0.08
  3. r = 0.74

A3.

Which statement about correlation is correct?

  1. Correlation implies causation.
  2. Correlation only captures linear association and is unitless.
  3. Correlation is measured in units of y.

Part B: Interpreting regression equation

Suppose the fitted line is:

\[\hat{y} = 42.5 + 3.1x\]

B1.

Interpret the slope:

  1. For each 1-unit increase in x, predicted mean y increases by about 3.1 units.
  2. When x = 0, predicted y is 3.1.
  3. The model explains 3.1% of variation in y.

B2.

Interpret the intercept:

  1. For each 1-unit increase in x, predicted mean y changes by 42.5.
  2. Predicted mean y when x = 0 is 42.5 (if meaningful in context).
  3. The minimum possible value of y is 42.5.

B3.

Use the model to predict y when x = 10.

  1. 42.5 + 3.1(10) = 73.5
  2. 42.5 + 10 = 52.5
  3. 3.1 + 10 = 13.1

Part C: Residuals, train/test MSE, and test (R^2)

C1.

A residual is:

  1. predicted - observed
  2. observed - predicted
  3. observed / predicted

C2.

Why is training MSE often optimistically biased?

  1. It is measured on the same data used to fit the model.
  2. Test sets always have data-entry errors.
  3. MSE cannot be computed on training data.

C3.

Which metric is usually better for expected performance on new data?

  1. Training MSE
  2. Test MSE
  3. Training residual mean

C4.

A model has test R^2 = 0.64. Best interpretation:

  1. 64% of variability in x is explained by y.
  2. About 64% of variability in test-set y is explained by linear prediction from x.
  3. 64% of individual predictions are exact.

Part D: Interpreting R output (summary(lm(...)))

Suppose R output includes:

  • Coefficients:
  • (Intercept) Estimate = 50.2
  • hours Estimate = 2.8
  • Pr(>|t|) for hours = 0.004
  • Multiple R-squared: 0.58

D1.

What does Estimate = 2.8 for hours mean?

  1. Predicted mean outcome increases by about 2.8 units per +1 hour in x.
  2. Outcome is always exactly 2.8.
  3. Correlation equals 2.8.

D2.

How should you interpret Pr(>|t|) = 0.004 for hours?

  1. Strong evidence slope differs from 0 (under model assumptions).
  2. Exactly 0.4% of observations are errors.
  3. Probability the null is true is 0.004.

D3.

How should you interpret Multiple R-squared: 0.58?

  1. 58% of x is explained by y.
  2. About 58% of variation in y in the fitted sample is explained by the linear model.
  3. 58% chance the model is correct.

Part E: Confidence intervals and prediction intervals

E1. CI for slope

Suppose confint(fit, "hours") returns (1.2, 4.3).

Best interpretation:

  1. We are 95% confident the population slope is between 1.2 and 4.3.
  2. 95% of points lie between 1.2 and 4.3.
  3. The slope equals exactly 2.75 with certainty.

E2. CI for mean response

predict(fit, newdata = data.frame(hours = 8), interval = "confidence") gives:

  • fit = 72.1, lwr = 69.8, upr = 74.4

Interpretation:

  1. 95% CI for mean outcome among all units with hours = 8 is 69.8 to 74.4.
  2. 95% of individual outcomes at hours = 8 must be in 69.8 to 74.4.
  3. 72.1 is the slope.

E3. Prediction interval for individual response

predict(fit, newdata = data.frame(hours = 8), interval = "prediction") gives:

  • fit = 72.1, lwr = 60.4, upr = 83.8

Best interpretation:

  1. 95% interval for one future individual outcome at hours = 8 is 60.4 to 83.8.
  2. 95% CI for the mean at hours = 8 is 60.4 to 83.8.
  3. The model is poor because the interval is wider than a mean-response CI.

E4.

Why is a prediction interval usually wider than a confidence interval for the mean at the same x0?

  1. It includes both uncertainty in the mean and individual-level variability.
  2. It uses a different unit system.
  3. It always uses a larger sample size.

Part F: Assumptions and diagnostics

F1.

Which condition is important for using usual t-based confidence intervals for slope in simple linear regression?

  1. Residuals are roughly normal (especially for small/moderate sample sizes).
  2. Explanatory variable is categorical.
  3. Response must be binary.

F2.

A residual histogram is strongly skewed with extreme outliers in a small sample. Best response:

  1. Interpret CIs/p-values cautiously; model assumptions may be questionable.
  2. Ignore diagnostics if R^2 is high.
  3. Conclude slope is definitely zero.

Part G: Short-response interpretation (1-3 sentences each)

G1.

Explain the difference between what test MSE and test R^2 each tell you.

G2.

In plain language, explain what it means when the 95% CI for slope excludes 0.

G3.

A student says: “Our training R^2 is high, so we are guaranteed to predict new data well.” Briefly respond.

G4.

You are given both a confidence interval for the mean at x0 and a prediction interval at the same x0. Which should you report for:

  • a population-average statement, and
  • an individual future-case statement?
