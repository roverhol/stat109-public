STAT 109: Introductory Biostatistics — Randomized controlled trials and inference for experiments

Lecture 29

Random assignment and what it buys you

Lecture 28 stayed in the observational world: three explanations for any group difference (causation, chance, confounding), DAGs and subclassification, and proportion-based inference plus randomization applets to quantify “by chance.” In an observational study, a small p-value does not pick between real causation and confounding.

This lecture focuses on randomized controlled trials (RCTs). Random assignment of the treatment is the design feature that replaces the worst confounding paths in expectation and narrows the main causal threats for internal validity—typically to real effect vs chance (plus issues like noncompliance, loss to follow-up, and measurement—we mention them but do not model them here).

Lecture 30 mixes RCT and observational examples in four capstone workflows.

Learning objectives

  • Describe an RCT and explain how random assignment supports causal conclusions about the assigned treatment (for participants in the trial, under good execution).
  • List two primary explanations for an arm-to-arm difference in an RCT (treatment effect, chance), and name at least one non-confounding threat (e.g. dropout) that still requires careful reading of a paper.
  • Distinguish random assignment and random sampling and say what each supports.
  • For a numerical outcome and two arms, compute Lecture 25 descriptives, plot boxplots / histograms, and run Welch’s two-sample t-test (t.test(..., var.equal = FALSE)) after checking conditions.
  • For a binary outcome and two arms, analyze 2 × 2 counts with prop.test() or chisq.test() and expected counts as in Lecture 28.
  • Write null and alternative hypotheses for population means or population proportions in words and symbols (Project 4).

RCT vs observational study (review)

RCT Observational (Lecture 28)
Who picks the group? Researchers randomly assign treatment/control. Units already belong to groups.
Causal claims? Stronger for effect of assignment on outcome, if trial is well run. Weak without extra assumptions; confounding stays on the table.
Explanations for a difference Mainly (1) effect (2) chance (+ execution issues). (1) effect (2) chance (3) confounding.

Internal validity in an RCT

Internal validity = how well the study rules out alternative explanations for an association within the trial.

Under ideal conditions, random assignment balances both measured and unmeasured baseline confounders on average, so arm-to-arm differences are less easily attributed to pre-existing differences than in Lecture 28’s university table.

Still think critically: blinding, adherence, attrition, and outcome measurement can break the simple story. A p-value does not prove “the treatment works in the world”—only “assignment is associated with outcome in this sample under modeling assumptions.”

Binary outcome in an RCT: infection by arm

Story (simulated). 200 patients per arm; count infections (yes/no).

Hypotheses (population proportions). Let \(p_V\) and \(p_P\) be the population probabilities (or long-run proportions) of infection under vaccine and placebo assignment in the target setting.

  • \(H_0\): \(p_V = p_P\) (no difference in infection probability between arms).
  • \(H_a\): \(p_V \neq p_P\) (two-sided), or a one-sided alternative if your protocol specifies.

The prop.test() and chisq.test() outputs test the no-association / equal-proportion null for a 2 × 2 table; interpret p-values as in Lecture 28 (here, under random assignment, a small p-value favors a treatment effect over chance, not confounding).

Code 
set.seed(2901)
vax_trial <- tibble(
  arm = factor(rep(c("Vaccine", "Placebo"), each = 200)),
  infected = c(rbinom(200, 1, 0.08), rbinom(200, 1, 0.14))
)

Bridge: when an RCT is impossible or unethical

Some exposures cannot be randomly assigned (e.g. long-term smoking in teens). Lecture 28’s DAG and observational tools return—the FEV activity (below) asks what goes wrong when people treat prediction like causation without design.

Optional class activity: FEV and smoking in children

Prompt. A student fits a model for lung function (FEV) and reports age, height, and sex are “significant” while smoking is not.

Discussion.

  1. Is the scientific goal prediction or a causal effect of smoking?
  2. Why is randomized assignment to smoking unethical? What does that force us back to?
  3. Draw a DAG with smoking, FEV, age, height, sex; debate which arrows belong.
  4. Overlap: very few young smokers → unstable comparisons in some subgroups—connect to Lecture 28 subclassification needing enough data in each stratum.

Rossman–Chance (optional reinforcement)

RCT p-values can also be taught with two-group randomization: the null hypothesis is “labels don’t matter”; under random assignment, that matches the physical randomization in the experiment.

## Binary outcome in an RCT: infection by arm

**Story (simulated).** 200 patients per arm; count **infections** (yes/no).

**Hypotheses (population proportions).** Let $p_V$ and $p_P$ be the **population** probabilities (or long-run proportions) of infection under **vaccine** and **placebo** assignment in the target setting.

-   $H_0$: $p_V = p_P$ (no difference in infection probability between arms).
-   $H_a$: $p_V \neq p_P$ (two-sided), or a one-sided alternative if your protocol specifies.

The **`prop.test()`** and **`chisq.test()`** outputs test the **no-association / equal-proportion** null for a **2 × 2** table; interpret *p*-values as in **Lecture 28** (here, under **random assignment**, a small *p*-value favors a **treatment effect** over **chance**, not confounding).

```{r}
set.seed(2901)
vax_trial <- tibble(
  arm = factor(rep(c("Vaccine", "Placebo"), each = 200)),
  infected = c(rbinom(200, 1, 0.08), rbinom(200, 1, 0.14))
)

tab_v <- table(vax_trial$arm, vax_trial$infected)
tab_v

infected_vaccine <- sum(vax_trial$infected[vax_trial$arm == "Vaccine"])
n_vaccine <- sum(vax_trial$arm == "Vaccine")
infected_placebo <- sum(vax_trial$infected[vax_trial$arm == "Placebo"])
n_placebo <- sum(vax_trial$arm == "Placebo")

prop.test(x = c(infected_vaccine, infected_placebo), n = c(n_vaccine, n_placebo), correct = FALSE)
chisq.test(tab_v)$expected
chisq.test(tab_v)
```

Compare **Lecture 28**: same **R** functions, but here **random assignment** makes a **low** infection rate in the vaccine arm **more** plausibly a **treatment effect** than the same table would be in an **observational** comparison.
